In 1788 Patrick Henry and Anti-Federalist were in control of the Virginia House of Delegates. They attempted to keep James Madison out of the U.S. House of Representatives by drawing the boundaries of Virginia's 5th Congressional District that favored James Monroe, however they were unsuccessful.

Elbridge Gerry was Governor of Massachusetts in 1812. He signed a bill that redistricted Massachuseets to favor the Democratic-Republican Party. When mapped one of the districts of Boston was thought to resemble a mythological salamander, thus the term gerrymandering was coined and printed for the first time in the Boston Gazette.

1896

Plessy v. Ferguson

Upheld the constitutionality of racial segregation, separate but equal is equal.

1954

Brown v. Board of Education

Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson, separate but equal is not equal. Resulted in schools being desegregated.

Colegrove v. Green

Three Illinois voters claimed states congressional districts lacked compactness and equal population, a violation of the constitution. The case was dismissed based on the grounds that the U.S. constitution placed no requirements on district plans regarding compactness, contiguity or equal population.

1946

Baker v. Carr

In Baker v. Carr a claim that Tennessee had failed to reappoint legislative districts in accordance with the state constitution. The district court dismissed the case, however, the Supreme Court reversed the ruling finding that legislature appoint was a justiciable issue not a political question. The court made it clear that any state who failed to apportion its districts in equal fashion is in violation of the Equal Protection Clause.

1962

Wesberry v. Sanders

James P. Wesberry brought the case against Georgia Governor Carl Sanders. Wesberry lived in a district whose population was two to three times larger than other districts within Georgia, which diluted the vote of the people within the district a violation of the U.S. Constitution. The court ruled in favor of Wesberry, districts need to be as equal in population as possible in order for everyone's vote to have the same weight.

1964

Voting Rights Act

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was signed into law by Present Lyndon B. Johnson. The goal of the Voting Rights Act was to give minority groups an equal opportunity to vote. It banned literacy tests and provided the federal oversight of voter registration in areas where less than half the non-white population was registered to vote. It also banned state redistricting that dilutes the vote of minority groups. It allowed the U.S. attorney general to investigate the use of poll taxes in state and local elections.

1965

Chicago School Boycott

More than 100,000 African-American students stayed home from school in protest of the renewed contract for Dr. Benjamin Willis, the white school superintendent. The boycott marked the beginning of the Chicago Freedom Movement (1965-1966) that lead to the arrival of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. in Chicago to lead the struggle of equal opportunities in education and housing.

1965

Chicago School Desegregation

Chicago Board of Education announced a preliminary plan to desegregate schools. The plan was denounced by Mayor Jane M. Byrne. The proposal called for the bussing of students and pairing of neighboring schools with differing demographic compositions in order to desegregate them. However, most white students had transferred to private school before the desegregation process began, meaning public schools in Chicago were mostly black.

1981

Thornburg v. Gingles

North Carolina passed a new district plan for the stateâ€™s Senate and House of Representatives. African Americans in North Carolina claimed that the new plan contained 7 new districts in which they would not be able to elect a representative of their choice; a violation of section 2 of the Voting Rights Act. The court found that five of the districts diluted the vote of African American Voters a violation of the Voting Rights Act. The court also established three criteria for proving racial vote dilution under the Voting Rights Act.

1986

Davis v. Bandemer

The case was brought by a group of Indiana Democrats who claimed that the appointment of Indiana State Legislatures diluted the voice of democrats voters in key districts, which is a violation of the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment of the Constitution. The Supreme Court rules Indiana's district plan was not illegally gerrymandered. The court maintained partisan gerrymandering cases are justiciable under the Equal Protection Clause.

1986

Miller v. Johnson

In Georgia 27% of the population was black, but they only had one congressional seat. The black population was entitled to another congressional seat. The congressional districts of Georgia had been drawn in such a way that the black population was only able to elect one black representative, which is racial gerrymandering. The court ruled racial gerrymandering is a violation of the Equal Protection Clause.

1995

Vieth v. Jubelirer

The case was brought by a group of Pennsylvania Democrats who claimed the state legislature, which was controlled by Republicans at the time of redistricting, had drawn a map that constituted an illegal partisan gerrymander. The court issued a split decision with no majority, as a result no precedent was established regarding partisan gerrymandering cases.

2004

REDMAP

In 2010 Republicans formulated a plan called REDMAP (Redistricting Majority Project). REDMAP looked for states that had a possibility of changed from blue to red states and redistricted them. In the 2010 midterm elections republicans gained 6 Senate seats and 63 House seats, making them the majority of the House of Representatives and gaining control over more state legislators than the Democrats had. The Republicans then started to work on redistricting wherever they could, this lead to a 23 seat gain in the House for Republicans in 2012 elections.

2010

Evenwell v. Abbott

The issue was the constitutionality of Texas legislative districts. The plaintiffs argued that district populations should only take into account the number or registered or eligible voters residing within those districts. The plantiffs argued that taking into account the total population in districts with a small population of non-eligible voters dilutes the voting power of other citizens in residing within the district. The court ruled that state or localities can use total population when redistricting.

2016

Rucho v. Common Cause

The court had ordered North Carolina to redraw some of its districts because it had been found that their districts map favored Republicans. The new map was redrawn by a Republican mapmaker who had instructions to ensure there was a 10-3 Republican majority in the congressional election. The map packed and cracked democratic voters in order to dilute their vote. North Caroline had gerrymandered its map in such a way that favored the Republican party. The Supreme Court ruled that partisan gerrymandering is nonjusticiable.
